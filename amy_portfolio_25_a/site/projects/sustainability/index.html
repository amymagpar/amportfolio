It started with the introduction of several new sustainability-focused data center products. Our tiger team was tasked to add customer-facing information about their sustainability and benefits but were given only marketing-focused copy.

Digging into user research revealed a troubling gap: only 20% of customers and internal teams knew where to find sustainability data or were aware of Cisco’s end-of-life programs. For a company committed to environmental responsibility, this wasn’t just an information gap—it was a missed opportunity for

Using Metrics to Drive Design

During stakeholder interviews, a product engineering sustainability manager shared a story that crystallized our challenge:

"I have two RFPs from sales due in 48 hours requiring power usage metrics for our networking solutions. I spent a day just trying to find if we have this data, only to discover it would take weeks to compile. We will lose the bids.”

This wasn't an isolated incident. I directed the analysis of UX research data, including interviews, surveys, and usability testing, to gain deep insights into user preferences and behavior. Our research revealed:

  • Marketing teams were surfacing generic sustainability content because they did not have product-specific data
  • Engineers had some of the data in a hard-to-use dashboard.
  • Sales teams were missing opportunities because they couldn't quickly access energy or manufacturing information.
  • Customers were making purchasing decisions without awareness of more lustainable options.

Key Insights

  1. Information Architecture Issues

    Our sustainabipty data was trapped in silos, buried in engineering dashboards, and scattered across different assets. Fragmented information placements and the absence of apppcable energy information hindered user engagement.

  2. Streamline Process Bottlenecks

    Manual data extraction takes 2-3 weeks,had multiple approval layers slowing response time and a lack of standardized sustainabiliy metrics

  3. Address Gaps in Content

    We lacked top-of-funnel sustainability content like Thought Leadership and infographics to educate customers. Middle-of-funnel content like Cisco-verified design guides needed to be created, and bottom-of-funnel content like energy use product-specific information and comparisons between products and competitors was not easily accessible.

  4. Surface Sustainability Information

    on purpose pages, while engaging Press and people researching Cisco, failed to drive users to product and solution pages, necessitating a strategic overhaul.

  5. Show, Don't Tell

    Instead of treating sustainability as a separate initiative, we needed to integrate it into every product story.

  6. Empower Sales, Marketing and customers

    with the information they need to choose more sustainable products and solutions. Give customers, partners, and other teams the information on proper disposal of End of Life products

Outcomes & Learnings

The key insight wasn't just about making data accessible—it was about making sustainability visible at every touchpoint where it mattered. By integrating environmental impact information into existing workflows and user journeys, we turned sustainability from an afterthought into a competitive differentiator.

This project taught us that sometimes the most impactful UX solutions aren't about creating new features—they're about surfacing hidden value in ways that naturally align with user needs.
